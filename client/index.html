The Pacific Front (December 7, 1941 – September 2, 1945)

General Info

Beginning with the Pearl Harbor attacks, the Pacific Theater was fought mainly between the U.S and Japan and covered a large part of the Pacfic Ocean and Southeast Asia. Japan was easily moving across China and Southeast Asia, but the Japanese awoke a sleeping giant when they attacked America: they did not complete all the objectives of Pearl Harbor, and they vastly underestimated the industrial and military power of the United States.

With the element of surprise, the Japanese enjoyed early military victories, but by mid-1942, the tide was starting to turn. After the battle of Midway, the Americans began to control the Pacific Ocean. This battle is considered the beginning of the end, and once American carriers were in range of the mainland, American bombers conducted devastating raids on Japanese cities, culminating in the two atomic bombs that forced Japan to surrender. While smaller than the European Front, the Pacific Theater was a brutal, bloody affair that resulted in over two million deaths directly caused by military action and had impacts that are still felt today.

This website explores the Pacific War in four aspects. The first being the attitudes of each nation toward the war. This includes the government's role as well as the ideas of "staying neutral" and Japanese imperialism. Also, there are categories for both land and naval battles, which also include the impact of technology and the conditions of the battles fought. Lastly, it explores the end of the war, which also includes the impacts beyond World War II itself.

American Views

America initially wanted to remain neutral in World War Two; they viewed the conflict as “Europe’s problem” and thought getting involved would only harm the U.S. American citizens. The government was not keen on getting involved in another potentially devastating conflict so soon after World War One. However, when Pearl Harbor was attacked, Americans were shocked and angered, and a poll a few days after the attack showed that 97% of Americans supported declaring war. War was declared immediately after the attack on December 8. First, however, America needed to intervene in Europe, as France had been taken over and Nazi Germany was advancing rapidly across Europe. America was taken by surprise and lost much of its territory in the Pacific, since it was not able to immediately deal with the Japanese threat. America was united against Japan, and men were drafted and encouraged to join the fight. Racism was also a factor in American hatred of the Japanese; not only was there general racism among the American population, but executive order 9066 rounded up over 120,000 Japanese-American citizens and placed them in internment camps. The government feared that some were secretly spies and were disloyal to the U.S.

How the War Started

Even before America entered World War Two and the Pacific War began, Japan attempted to gain territory in South and Southeast Asia to fuel its own territorial ambitions. Japan invaded China and made rapid gains, also annexing Korea and Manchuria, among others. However, Japan was not able to produce large quantities of important resources, such as rubber. During the 1930’s Japan grew even more aggressive and militaristic, looking down on other nations and becoming increasingly antagonistic towards America and its allies. Japan knew that it would eventually have to fight a war if it wanted to realize its ambitions, and it started to build battleships that violated treaties such as the 1922 Washington Naval Treaty. The Japanese navy gradually began mobilizing, and the first fleet was formed on April 10th, 1941. In July 1941, Japan invaded the southern part of French Indochina which prompted the U.S to impose an embargo on oil products to Japan. Japan particularly wanted the Dutch East Indies for their oilfields, in addition to the Philippines and Malaya. On December 7, 1941, after meticulous planning, Pearl Harbor was attacked. This brought the United States into the war, united against the Japanese and reeling from their initial losses.

Japanese Views

The Japanese were unhappy with the outcome of World War One; while they were on the winning side, they did not gain any territory and still had trouble producing important resources. The Japanese government slowly became more militaristic and totalitarian, and Emperor Hirohito was touted as a “living god.” Japanese schools taught that the Japanese were superior and other nations were subhuman. Children were highly encouraged to join the military, and training began in schools. Citizens were pressured or forced to either fight in the war or work as laborers. The Pearl Harbor attack was partly to realize a Japanese victory over the United States, and also as revenge for the U.S oil embargo and providing aid to the Chinese. When the war later became desperate for the Japanese, they resorted to kamikaze - suicide tactics - methods in order to win: they viewed surrender as highly dishonorable and it was expected that a soldier would sacrifice himself for victory and their emporer. Even before World War One, Japanese High Command had a list of potential enemies, of which one was the United States, showing that the Japanese were always weary and preparing for war in the future.

Major Land Battles

Battle of Attu
May 11-30, 1943
The Battle of Attu was the only land battle fought in North America during World War II.
On June 7, 1942, the Japanese invaded and occupied Attu. Attu was a remote island part of the Aleutian region in the North Pacific close to the Alaskan Peninsula It is thought that Japan seized Attu primarily to divert the U.S. during the attack on Midway Island (June 4–7, 1942) in the central Pacific. Americans were surprised that Japanese troops could take any U.S. land, no matter how remote or insignificant. Some Americans also feared that this might be the first step toward an attack against mainland Alaska or even the U.S. Northwest. On March 26, 1943, the U.S. Navy managed to secure the sea lanes and clear the way for an attack on Attu. Then, on May 11, 1943, in a mission code-named Operation Landgrab, the U.S. soldiers at first encountered only light resistance. Still, the island’s harsh weather and rugged terrain proved to be helpful for the Japanese. The U.S. Army planners had expected the battle to last only a few days and had not anticipated how grueling the conditions would be, American soldiers conducted operations in substandard clothing with inadequate gear. However, the Americans were supported by naval and aerial bombardment against Japanese positions, and aided by reinforcements and new supplies. By the end of May, U.S. troops had managed to take the high ground on the island and trap Yamasaki’s troops in a small hillside area where they were quickly running out of food and ammunition. The remaining Japanese soldiers, mostly adhered to the traditional Bushido code, “way of the warrior”, forbidding surrender as the ultimate dishonor, began to commit suicide or prepare for the final charge. On May 29, 1943, Yamasaki and his remaining troops charged the American position in one of the largest banzai charges (all out desperate attack) of the Pacific War. Their sudden frontal assault on the initially penetrated all the way to caught-off guard support troops in the rear of the American camp. Brutal hand-to-hand combat followed until Yamasaki and his men were finally routed by overwhelming firepower.
Battle of Okinawa
April 1-June 21, 1945
The Battle of Okinawa was the last major battle before the atomic bombs were dropped.
By the time American troops landed on Okinawa, the war on the European front was nearing its end. In the Pacific theater, however, American forces were still suffering conquering Japan’s Home Islands, one after another. After winning the brutal Battle of Iwo Jima, they turned to the island of Okinawa, the last stop before reaching Japan. The Japanese knew if Okinawa fell, so would Japan. The Americans knew securing Okinawa’s air bases was crucial to finishing the war. On April 1, 1945, morale was low among American troops, but they still launched the largest bombardment ever to support a troop landing. Troops landing on Okinawa’s beaches surged inland with little resistance. Wave after wave of troops, tanks, ammunition and supplies went ashore almost effortlessly within hours. The troops quickly secured the two airfields. However, the Japanese army was waiting for them. As they progressed deeper, American troops wondered when and where they’d finally encounter resistance. The Japanese Imperial Army had them right where they wanted them. To defend the island, Japanese troops hunkered down in a network of caves and dugouts. Much of the fighting was hand-to-hand and particularly ruthless. The Americans finally took Hacksaw Ridge on May 6. To encourage their surrender, General Buckner initiated propaganda warfare and dropped millions of leaflets declaring the war was all but lost for Japan. When faced with the reality that further fighting was futile, General Ushijima and his Chief of Staff, General Cho, committed ritual suicide on June 22, effectively ending the Battle of Okinawa. Winning the Battle of Okinawa put Allied forces within striking distance of Japan. But wanting to bring the war to a swift end, and knowing there were 2 million Japanese troops waiting for the American soldiers, President Truman decided to drop two atomic bombs.

Harsh Jungle Conditions

Land battles in the Pacific Theater were fought mainly in the jungles of southeast asia, where soldiers faced numerous environmental hazards in addition to the normal dangers of fighting a war. Allied soldiers had to contend with heat constantly above ninety degrees and sometimes one-hundred degrees fahrenheit. The thick fog and heavy rains made it difficult to travel and identify enemy positions, and the tropical environment was a breeding ground for mosquitoes. Troops faced the danger of numerous diseases, including dengue fever, typhus, and jungle rot. It was common for a wound to become infected, and both sides lost more men from disease and exposure than they did in battle.

Japanese Treatment of Allied Soldiers

The Bataan Death March. (Painting by Mukai Junkichi)
Allied soldiers captured by the Japanese could not expect to be treated well or even to survive for very long. In addition to their using their prisoners for forced labor and their brutal attitude towards warfare, the Japanese viewed surrender as the height of dishonor, and looked down on enemy soldiers that surrendered, viewing them as cowardly and less than human for surrendering instead of dying. They therefore did not always see an issue with mistreating enemy captives, and American POWs felt the consequences with high casualty rates. The Bataan death march is one of the most well-known instances of Japanese mistreatment of enemy POWs. The Japanese immediately invaded the Philippines after Pearl Harbor, and the Bataan province fell in April of 1942. Over 60,000 American and Filipino soldiers that survived the fight were taken to a Japanese prison camp where they were tortured and shown no mercy by Japanese soldiers; during which several hundred soldiers died. A newspaper published in San Francisco in 1942 examines the horrors that the soldiers faced on the march; “the guards clubbed prisoners who tried to get water, and one prisoner was clubbed for helping a fellow prisoner who had been knocked down by a Japanese army truck. Soldiers were tortured or killed for trying to find food or escape. When the soldiers were finally liberated, a further 2,200 Americans and 20,000 Filipinos died at Camp O’Donnell, a U.S army camp that housed the survivors.

Important Technology

One new weapon of war used to great effect during the Pacific theater, seeing its first successful use on January 15, 1943. The flamethrower was invented at the beginning of the 20th century, but reached the point where it could be used in combat in the early 1940s. The weapon was extremely useful at clearing enemy bunkers which defied normal sidearms, making it easier to assault fortified enemy positions and reducing casualties in the process.
A soldier from the 33rd Infantry Division uses an M2 flamethrower
The Higgins boat, invented by Andrew Higgins, featured a new hull that prevented propeller grounding and allowed amphibious assaults with far fewer casualties than would have occurred otherwise. This significantly reduced the amount of soldiers and supplies needed to storm an enemy beach, which was very useful for fighting on the island chains in the Pacific. Indeed, amphibious assaults were used to great effect throughout the Pacific front; they were the main form of attack in ship-to-shore warfare, where ground forces received extensive support from nearby naval guns. Engineers were used extensively in these attacks as they had experience crossing rivers and defusing mines. The U.S conducted 39 major amphibious assaults in the Pacific theater.

Major Naval Battles

Attack on Pearl Harbor
Dec 7, 1941
This launched America into World War II.
Pearl Harbor is a U.S. naval base near Honolulu, Hawaii, where the Japanese launched a surprise attack. Just before 8 a.m., hundreds of Japanese fighter planes descended on the base, where they managed to destroy/damage almost 20 American naval vessels and over 300 airplanes. More than 2,400 Americans died in the attack, including civilians, and another 1,000 people were wounded. The day after the assault, President Franklin D. Roosevelt asked Congress to declare war on Japan. The attack on Pearl Harbor was a surprise, but Japan and the United States had been edging toward a full-out war for decades. The United States was unhappy with Japan’s attempts to invade China. Japan declared war on China in 1937, resulting in the Nanking Massacre and other awful events. American officials responded economically. They reasoned that if Japan lacked access to money and goods, and essential supplies like oil, Japan would have slow down. Pearl Harbor, Hawaii, is located near the center of the Pacific Ocean, roughly 2,000 miles from the U.S. mainland and about 4,000 miles from Japan. No one believed that the Japanese would start a war with an attack on the distant islands of Hawaii. American intelligence was confident that any Japanese attack would take place in one of the nearby European colonies in the South Pacific. Because American military leaders were not expecting an attack so close to home, the naval facilities at Pearl Harbor were relatively undefended. From Japan's point of view, Pearl Harbor was an irresistibly easy target. The Japanese plan was to destroy the Pacific Fleet. That way, the Americans would not be able to fight back as Japan’s forces spread across the South Pacific. On December 7, after months of planning and practice, the Japanese launched their attack. Less than two hours later, the surprise attack was over, and every battleship in Pearl Harbor—USS Arizona, USS Oklahoma, USS California, USS West Virginia, USS Utah, USS Maryland, USS Pennsylvania, USS Tennessee and USS Nevada—had sustained significant damage. However, the Japanese had failed to cripple the Pacific Fleet. By the 1940s, battleships were no longer the most important naval vessel: it was actually the aircraft carries, luckily, all of the Pacific Fleet’s carriers were away from the base on that fated day. Moreover, the Pearl Harbor assault had left the base’s repair buildings intact. As a result, the U.S. Navy was able to rebound relatively quickly from the attack. President Franklin D. Roosevelt addressed a joint session of the U.S. Congress on December 8, the day after the crushing attack on Pearl Harbor. He said, “No matter how long it may take us to overcome this premeditated invasion, the American people in their righteous might will win through to absolute victory. I believe I interpret the will of the Congress and of the people when I assert that we will not only defend ourselves to the uttermost, but will make very certain that this form of treachery shall never endanger us again.” The Japanese only wanted to coax the United States into an agreement to lift the economic sanctions against them; but it backfired, they had spurred America into a global conflict.
Battle of Midway
June 4-7, 1942
This battle is considered the turning point of the Pacific Theatre.
The Battle of Midway was a huge clash between the U.S. Navy and the Imperial Japanese Navy. Japan’s efforts to establish clear naval and air superiority in the western Pacific first hit a snag in the Battle of the Coral Sea in May 1942, when the U.S. fleet turned back a Japanese invasion force headed for New Guinea. Despite the setback, Admiral Isaroku Yamamoto, commander of the Imperial Japanese Navy, was convinced his forces enjoyed a numerical advantage over the Americans. U.S. Navy cryptanalysts had begun breaking Japanese communication codes early in 1942, and knew well ahead of time that Japan was planning an attack in the Pacific at a location they suspected was Midway. The Navy decided to send out a false message from the base claiming it was short of fresh water. Admiral Yamamoto had to transmit all strategy over the radio, enabling Navy cryptanalysts based in Hawaii to figure out when Japan planned to attack. Before dawn the next day, Japan launched the first phase of its attack as planned, sending 108 Japanese warplanes from its four aircraft carriers to strike Midway. After inflicting heavy damage to the U.S. base, the first attack ended by 7 a.m., but the airfield was still functional. Shortly after that, U.S. aircraft launched from Midway and began attacking the four Japanese carriers, to no avail. Meanwhile, a wave of U.S. Devastator torpedo bombers from the U.S. carriers Hornet and Enterprise arrived to attack the Japanese ships. Nearly all of them were shot down by Japanese Zero fighters because they were unescorted by fighter planes. But about an hour later another wave of U.S. carrier-launched bombers struck, crippling three Japanese carriers: Akagi, Kaga and Soryu. Japan’s surviving carrier, Hiryu, launched attacks but the U.S. dive-bombers from all three carriers returned to attack the Hiryu and set it destroy it as well, putting all four Japanese carriers out of commission. Major combat in the Battle of Midway was over by the evening of June 4, but U.S. troops at sea and on Midway Island continued their attacks on the Japanese over the next two days. On June 6, Yamamoto ordered his ships to retreat, ending the Battle of Midway. In short, Japan had lost as many as 3,000 men, nearly 300 aircraft, one heavy cruiser and four aircraft carriers in the battle, while the Americans lost two aircraft carriers, around 145 aircraft, and approximately 360 servicemen. As a result of the U.S. victory in the Battle of Midway, Japan abandoned its plan to expand its reach in the Pacific, and was put on the defensive. The battle injected U.S. forces with confidence and drained Japanese morale, turning the tide of war in the Pacific signifigantly in favor of the Allies.

Suicide Tactics

The kamikaze suicide pilot was Japan’s most ruthless weapon. The pilots were instructed to dive their planes into ships and cause as much destruction as possible. They were under the impression that they were dying in the most honorable way. On April 4, 1945, the Japanese unleashed these well-trained pilots on the Fifth Fleet. Some dove their planes into ships at speeds of 500 miles per hour causing immense damage. American sailors tried desperately to shoot the kamikaze planes down but were often sitting ducks against enemy pilots willing to die with their planes. There was essentially nothing that could be done against someone with nothing to lose.

Japanese Desperation

At the end of 1944, the Japanese were in dire straits; they were nearly out of much needed supplies and natural resources, like oil, and the majority of their navy had been lost to America’s much larger navy and air force. It was clear that the Japanese were going to lose, and in April of 1943, MacArthur was appointed commander of all U.S ground forces, and admiral Nimitiz was appointed commander of all U.S naval forces in order to expedite a potential invasion of Japan. In July of 1945, Truman called for Japan’s surrender, calling for “prompt and utter destruction” if they did not. Throughout the summer of 1945, American bombs and guns hammered on the Japanese coast, preparing for a potential invasion that would end in 1946. Even with most of Japan’s forces decimated, the invasion was going to be extremely costly. Japanese troops received orders to fight to the last man, and to them, death was infinitely preferable to surrender. This was present in the liberal use of suicide tactics in both naval and ground combat, and in the battle of Okinawa 110,000 Japanese troops were killed, while less than 8,000 were taken prisoner. The commanding general and his staff committed seppuku so as not to be taken prisoner by the Americans. American troops had to destroy each Japanese fortification singly, and the Japanese did not surrender under nearly the last possible second. America wanted unconditional surrender, and die-hard sections of the Japanese government believed that unconditional surrender would be the equivalent of mass destruction. The most conservative estimates for an invasion of Japan estimated the U.S would suffer over 225,000 casualties. Even after the first atomic bomb, emperor Hirohito refused to surrender.
Seppuku was a ritual form of suicide used by samurai warriors to avoid surrender or atone for a shameful act

Decision to Drop the Bomb

On July 16, 1945, the first atomic bomb, Trinity, was successfully detonated. The primary reason for dropping the atomic bomb was to encourage Japanese surrender and save American lives, knowing that millions of American soldiers and Japanese citizens would be lost in the invasion of Japan. However, there are other potential reasons why Truman may have wanted to drop the bomb instead of invading. The Soviet Union was also at war with Japan, and Truman knew that Japan would have capitulated upon the entry of the Russians into the war, significantly reducing the number of American casualties in a potential invasion. The bomb was seen as a way to end the war before the Soviet’s entered Japan, meaning the territory and spoils of victory were America’s alone. America wanted to end the war before the Soviet Union could grab pieces of the Asian mainland, and the bombs were not necessary but were used to accelerate the surrender process and force divided elements of the Japanese government to see reason. Racism could have made it easier to drop the bombs as well, as Americans likely did not see it as a crime; most Americans supported the bomb droppings without thinking of the moral consequences, and America’s hatred for the Japanese and the practice of total war made the decision very easy.

Future Impacts of the War

The atomic bomb was not just a cutting-edge weapon that led to the instant death of tens of thousands of Japanese civilians; there were lasting impacts on both Japanese citizens and on the world as a whole. Japanese citizens suffered radiation poisoning and genetic diseases that are still present today, and far more suffered as a result of the bombs than those that died in the first year. The Japanese were scared of these weapons, and surrendered partly out of shock. The emperor of Japan said that “the enemy used cruel bombs.” More importantly, never before did mankind have the capacity to destroy itself. With atomic weapons, the damage a war could do increased drastically, and countries became afraid of fighting other nuclear powers. The primary super-power opposing the United States was the Soviet Union, and even though the two countries had worked together to fight a common enemy in World War 2, they now opposed one another, each holding very different ideologies. America came out of the war as the dominant western superpower, now effectively in control of Japan for the time being, and they faced the Soviet Union, the only superpower after the war strong enough to rival the United States. Two years later, a different kind of war, The Cold War, would begin with the Soviet Union.
