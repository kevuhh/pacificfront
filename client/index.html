The Pacific Front

General Info

Beginning with the Pearl Harbor attacks, the Pacific Theater was fought mainly between the U.S and Japan and covered a large part of the Pacfic Ocean and Southeast Asia. Japan was easily moving across China and Southeast Asia, but the Japanese awoke a sleeping giant when they attacked America: they did not complete all the objectives of Pearl Harbor, and they vastly underestimated the industrial and military power of the United States.

With the element of surprise, the Japanese enjoyed early military victories, but by mid-1942, the tide was starting to turn. After the battle of Midway, the Americans began to control the Pacific Ocean. This battle is considered the beginning of the end, and once American carriers were in range of the mainland, American bombers conducted devastating raids on Japanese cities, culminating in the two atomic bombs that forced Japan to surrender. While smaller than the European Front, the Pacific Theater was a brutal, bloody affair that resulted in over two million deaths directly caused by military action and had impacts that are still felt today.

This website explores the Pacific War in four aspects. The first being the attitudes toward the war of each nation. This includes the government's role as well as the ideas of "staying neutral" and Japanese imperialism. Also, there are categories for both land and naval battles, which also include the impact of technology and the conditions of the battles fought. Lastly, it explores the end of the war, which also includes the impacts beyond World War II itself.

American Views

America initially wanted to remain neutral in World War Two; they viewed the conflict as “Europe’s problem” and thought getting involved would only harm the U.S. American citizens and the government were not keen on getting involved in another potentially devastating conflict so soon after World War One. When Pearl Harbor was attacked, Americans were shocked and angered, and a poll a few days after the attack showed that 97% of Americans supported declaring war. War was declared immediately after the attack on December 8. First, however, America needed to intervene in Europe, as France had been taken over and Nazi Germany was advancing rapidly across Europe. America was taken by surprise and lost much of its territory in the Pacific, but was not able to immediately deal with the Japanese threat. America was united against Japan, and men were drafted and encouraged to join the fight. Racism was also a factor in American hatred of the Japanese; not only was there general racism among the American population, but executive order 9066 rounded up over 120,000 Japanese-American citizens and placed them in internment camps. The government feared that some were secretly spies and were disloyal to the U.S.

How the War Started

Even before America entered World War Two and the Pacific War began, Japan attempted to gain territory in South and Southeast Asia to fuel its own territorial ambitions. Japan invaded China and made rapid gains, also annexing Korea and Manchuria, among others. However, Japan was not able to produce large quantities of important resources, such as rubber. During the 1930’s Japan grew even more aggressive and militaristic, looking down on other nations and becoming increasingly antagonistic towards America and its allies. Japan knew that it would eventually have to fight a war if it wanted to realize its ambitions, and it started to build battleships that violated treaties such as the 1922 Washington Naval Treaty. The Japanese navy gradually began mobilizing, and the first fleet was formed on April 10th, 1941. In July 1941, Japan invaded the southern part of French Indochina which prompted the U.S to impose an embargo on oil products to Japan. Japan particularly wanted the Dutch East Indies for their oilfields, in addition to the Philippines and Malaya. On December 7, 1941, after meticulous planning, Pearl Harbor was attacked. This brought the United States into the war, united against the Japanese and reeling from their initial losses.

Japanese Views

The Japanese were unhappy with the outcome of World War One; while they were on the winning side, they did not gain any territory and still had trouble producing important resources. The Japanese government slowly became more militaristic and totalitarian, and Emperor Hirohito was touted as a “living god.” Japanese schools taught that the Japanese were superior and other nations were subhuman. Children were highly encouraged to join the military, and training began in schools. Citizens were pressured or forced to either fight in the war or work as laborers. The Pearl Harbor attack was partly to realize a Japanese victory over the United States, and also as revenge for the U.S oil embargo and providing aid to the Chinese. When the war later became desperate for the Japanese, they resorted to kamikaze - suicide tactics - methods in order to win: they viewed surrender as highly dishonorable and it was expected that a soldier would sacrifice himself for victory and they emporer. Even before World War One, Japanese High Command had a list of potential enemies, of which one was the United States, showing that the Japanese were always weary and preparing for war in the future.

Major Land Battles

Harsh Jungle Conditions

Land battles in the Pacific Theater were fought mainly in the jungles of southeast asia, where soldiers faced numerous environmental hazards in addition to the normal dangers of fighting a war. Allied soldiers had to contend with heat constantly above ninety degrees and sometimes one-hundred degrees fahrenheit. Squads faced numerous diseases, including dengue fever, typhus, and jungle rot.

Japanese Treatment of Allied Soldiers

The Bataan Death March. (Painting by Mukai Junkichi)
Allied soldiers captured by the Japanese could not expect to be treated well or even to survive for very long. In addition to their using their prisoners for forced labor and their brutal attitude towards warfare, the Japanese viewed surrender as the height of dishonor, and looked down on enemy soldiers that surrendered, viewing them as cowardly and less than human for surrendering instead of dying. They therefore did not always see an issue with mistreating enemy captives, and American POWs felt the consequences with high casualty rates. The Bataan death march is one of the most well-known instances of Japanese mistreatment of enemy POWs. The Japanese immediately invaded the Philippines after Pearl Harbor, and the Bataan province fell in April of 1942. Over 60,000 American and Filipino soldiers that survived the fight were taken to a Japanese prison camp where they were tortured and shown no mercy by Japanese soldiers; during which several hundred soldiers died. A newspaper published in San Francisco in 1942 examines the horrors that the soldiers faced on the march; “the guards clubbed prisoners who tried to get water, and one prisoner was clubbed for helping a fellow prisoner who had been knocked down by a Japanese army truck. Soldiers were tortured or killed for trying to find food or escape. When the soldiers were finally liberated, a further 2,200 Americans and 20,000 Filipinos died at Camp O’Donnell, a U.S army camp that housed the survivors.
